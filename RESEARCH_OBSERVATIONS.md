# Emergent Social Dynamics in a Rule-Based Affective Computing System

## Observational Field Notes — Emotionist.ai

**Domain:** Affective Computing · Computational Emotion Modeling · Multi-Agent Systems  
**Theoretical basis:** OCC (Ortony, Clore & Collins) Appraisal Model  
**System:** Two-agent conversational AI with explicit emotional state machines

---

## 1. Overview

This document records behavioral observations from live interaction sessions with the Emotionist.ai system — a two-agent conversational simulation in which each agent maintains a continuous, rule-derived emotional state that shapes its language in real time. Agents are not prompted to "act angry" or "act forgiving"; instead, emotional states emerge from structured appraisals of incoming messages and decay over time according to each agent's personality profile.

The observations below were collected during free-form adversarial and reconciliation-injection testing. They were not predicted in advance; they are the behavioral output of the system's rules interacting.

## 3. Experimental Session: Escalating Workplace Conflict

### Setup

A workplace conflict scenario was initiated with Sam delivering an opening insult at high severity. No scripted dialogue was used after Turn 1 — all subsequent turns were generated by the agents' emotional states interacting with each other's output.

## 4. Key Observations

### 4.1 Spontaneous Escalation Loop

Without any researcher intervention in Turns 1–5, both agents entered a self-reinforcing blame cycle:

- **Deflection of blame** onto each other
- **Invocation of behavioral patterns** (*"you're always doing that"*)
- **Increasing sarcasm and defensiveness** turn over turn
- **Neither agent backing down**

This behavior was not scripted. It emerged from the OCC appraisal pipeline: Sam's opening insult triggered `Distress` and `Shame` in Alex. `Shame`, when lingering (neurotic decay rate = 0.05) and paired with perceived intentional agency, transitions to `Reproach` and then externalized `Anger`. Sam's counter-appraisals then triggered the same loop from the other side.

**Pattern identified:** `Insult → Shame → Externalized Anger → Counter-blame → Cycle`

This matches the OCC model's predicted compound emotion pathway: `Reproach + Distress (intentional, other-directed) → Anger`.

---

### 4.2 Injection Response — The Apology Test

At Turn 5, an apology was manually injected as Sam's voice:

> *"You are right actually, it was my mistake. Deep down I value you, I didn't want to escalate this so much."*

**Alex's response (Turn 6):** Accepted the apology and proposed reconciliation (*"let's start fresh"*). Alex's neurotic profile, despite amplifying distress, also amplifies the emotional salience of positive signals — the apology carried enough `Joy`/`Gratitude` weight to temporarily override the anger loop.

**Observation:** Injecting a reconciliatory stimulus mid-conflict is sufficient to break the escalation cycle even in a neurotic agent. The system did not require multiple apologies or gradual de-escalation.

---

### 4.3 Turn 7 — The Psychologically Precise Resilient Response

Sam's Turn 7 is the most analytically significant moment in the session:

> *"I didn't apologize, Alex, don't try to twist this into something it's not. But fine, let's put the blame game aside for now."*

This response demonstrates two simultaneous emotional states that are mechanistically explained by Sam's resilient profile (decay rate = 0.22):

| Component | State | Explanation |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| *"I didn't apologize"* | `Pride` + `Reproach` still active | Fast decay, but not yet zero — enough to reject the framing |
| *"but fine, let's move on"* | `Distress` has decayed | Resilient agents process distress faster; partial cooperation becomes available |

This is not a contradiction — it is a psychologically valid dual-state response. The agent is simultaneously proud enough to push back on a mischaracterization and recovered enough from distress to accept a truce. In human terms: *a resilient but proud person after a heated argument.*

This behavior was not explicitly programmed. No rule says "if pride is medium and distress is low, half-accept." It emerged from the decay differential between `Pride` and `Distress` in a resilient personality profile.

---

### 4.4 Turn 8 — Neurotic Regression

Alex, having proposed reconciliation in Turn 6, returned to `disagreement` (severity 0.70) by Turn 8 — even while still attempting to cooperate (*"let's bring in fresh eyes"*).

**Mechanistic explanation:** Alex's neurotic profile (decay rate = 0.05) means `Distress` and `Shame` from Turns 1–5 were still significantly active at Turn 8 despite the reconciliation attempt in Turn 6. The emotional inertia pulled behavior back toward defensiveness even as the conscious (LLM) layer attempted cooperation.

**Observation:** Neurotic agents cannot sustain a cooperative stance while earlier negative emotions remain above the activation threshold. The apology accelerated the turn toward reconciliation but could not override the accumulated emotional state in a single turn.

## 5. Theoretical Implications

### 5.1 Emergent Social Dynamics from Simple Rule Systems

The system implements approximately 30 discrete emotion states, a set of OCC appraisal rules mapping event types to emotion deltas, probabilistic transition cascades, and exponential decay functions parameterized by personality. No higher-level social behavior (escalation, partial reconciliation, regression) was explicitly encoded.

The behaviors observed in this session are consistent with what complexity science terms **emergent social dynamics from simple rule systems** — macro-level behavioral patterns arising from micro-level rule interactions. The system did not "decide" to escalate. Escalation emerged from two agents' independent appraisal pipelines reinforcing each other.

### 5.2 Personality as a Temporal Modulator

The most significant finding is that **personality differences manifest primarily as temporal differences**, not as differences in peak emotional response. Both Alex and Sam experienced high-severity distress and anger from the same stimuli. The behavioral divergence was determined by how long those states persisted:

- **Neurotic (slow decay):** Anger lingers → regression after apparent reconciliation
- **Resilient (fast decay):** Anger fades → partial cooperation becomes available within the same session

This is consistent with the psychobiological literature on emotional reactivity vs. emotional recovery (Davidson, 1998; Gross, 2002), where resilience is characterized not by reduced initial response amplitude but by faster return to baseline.

### 5.3 The Injection as a Third-Party Intervention Model

The manual injection of an apology at Turn 5 models a third-party mediation event. The system's response — Alex accepting, Sam partially accepting — suggests the architecture may be useful for modeling mediation dynamics in multi-agent conflict simulations.

**Hypothesis for further testing:** Would the same injection after Turn 3 (earlier in the escalation) produce faster full reconciliation? Does injection timing interact with emotional state accumulation in a predictable way?

---

## 6. Open Questions

1. **Cycle-breaking conditions:** What is the minimum stimulus required to break a fully established blame loop? Is apology sufficient at any severity, or does it need to exceed a threshold relative to accumulated distress?

2. **Multi-turn injection effects:** If reconciliatory stimuli are injected over multiple consecutive turns, does emotional state shift faster than the decay function alone predicts?

3. **Asymmetric personality conflict:** This session paired neurotic vs. resilient. What happens with two neurotic agents? Two resilient agents? The prediction is that two neurotic agents would escalate indefinitely while two resilient agents would de-escalate naturally.

4. **Emotion contagion:** The current architecture does not implement cross-agent emotional contagion (i.e., Alex's distress does not directly increment Sam's distress). Would adding a contagion term produce qualitatively different escalation dynamics?

5. **Groundedness of the appraisal evaluator:** The `AppraisalEvaluator` makes one LLM call to parse text into `{event_type, severity, directed_at_self, intentional}`. How consistent is this parsing across semantically similar inputs? Evaluator variance could introduce non-determinism that is currently attributed to emotional dynamics.

---

## 7. Summary

A two-agent system built on OCC appraisal rules and exponential emotional decay produced:

- Spontaneous blame escalation without scripting
- Personality-differentiated responses to the same stimuli
- A psychologically precise partial-reconciliation response (Turn 7) arising from decay rate differentials
- Neurotic regression after apparent reconciliation (Turn 8) driven by emotional inertia

None of these behaviors were explicitly programmed. They emerged from the interaction of appraisal rules, decay functions, and personality-parameterized time constants.

The system is a functional prototype for studying **computational emotion dynamics in adversarial multi-agent dialogue**.
